# Landing Page CRO Audit Checklist

A comprehensive checklist with 80+ checkpoints for auditing landing page conversion performance.
Each checkpoint includes a description, why it matters, how to fix it, and expected impact level.

Use this checklist to systematically evaluate any landing page. Score each item as:
- **Pass** (2 points): Fully implemented and optimized
- **Partial** (1 point): Present but needs improvement
- **Fail** (0 points): Missing or poorly implemented

**Overall Page Health Score**: Total points / (Total checkpoints x 2) x 100

---

## Section 1: Above the Fold (15 Checkpoints)

The above-the-fold area receives ~80% of visitor attention. These are the highest-impact checkpoints.

### 1.1 Clear, Benefit-Driven Headline
- **Description**: The headline communicates a specific benefit the visitor will receive, not a feature description or company name.
- **Why it matters**: The headline is the most-read element on the page. 80% of visitors read the headline, but only 20% read further. A benefit-driven headline keeps them reading.
- **How to fix**: Rewrite using the formula: "[Get/Achieve] [specific result] [in timeframe] [without pain point]". Example: "Cut Your Reporting Time by 80% in the First Week."
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — headline changes can increase conversions 10-30%.

### 1.2 Supporting Subheadline
- **Description**: A subheadline that expands the headline with specifics (how it works, for whom, or a supporting benefit).
- **Why it matters**: The subheadline catches visitors who need more detail before deciding to scroll. It bridges the headline to the CTA.
- **How to fix**: Add 15-25 words that answer "How?" or "For whom?" with specific details or numbers.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — subheadline improvements typically lift conversions 5-10%.

### 1.3 Single, Prominent Primary CTA
- **Description**: One primary call-to-action button is clearly visible without scrolling. No competing CTAs.
- **Why it matters**: Multiple CTAs above the fold create decision paralysis. One clear action reduces friction.
- **How to fix**: Remove secondary CTAs from the hero. Keep only the primary conversion action.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — reducing CTA options can increase clicks 20-30%.

### 1.4 CTA Button Contrast
- **Description**: The CTA button color has sufficient contrast against the background and is the most visually prominent element.
- **Why it matters**: If the CTA does not stand out, visitors may not notice it. The button should "pop" against the page.
- **How to fix**: Use a high-contrast color not used elsewhere on the page. Test with the squint test — squint at the page and the CTA should still be visible.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — CTA color changes can lift clicks 5-15%.

### 1.5 Benefit-Driven CTA Copy
- **Description**: Button text communicates the value of clicking, not the action. "Get more leads" not "Submit".
- **Why it matters**: Generic CTA text ("Submit", "Click Here", "Sign Up") creates friction because it focuses on what the visitor has to do, not what they get.
- **How to fix**: Rewrite CTA copy using first person ("Start my free trial") or outcome language ("Get instant access").
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — CTA copy changes typically lift conversions 5-15%.

### 1.6 Social Proof Above the Fold
- **Description**: At least one element of social proof is visible without scrolling (user count, logo bar, rating, or testimonial snippet).
- **Why it matters**: Social proof above the fold reduces the visitor's initial anxiety. It signals "Others trust this, so can I."
- **How to fix**: Add a one-liner: "Trusted by 10,000+ teams" or a mini logo bar of 4-6 recognizable companies.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — adding social proof above the fold can increase conversions 10-20%.

### 1.7 Relevant Hero Image or Visual
- **Description**: A hero image, screenshot, video, or illustration that supports the headline and shows the product or outcome.
- **Why it matters**: Visuals are processed 60,000x faster than text. The right image reinforces the value proposition instantly.
- **How to fix**: Use a product screenshot showing the key benefit in action, or an image of the desired outcome.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — relevant visuals can improve conversions 5-15%.

### 1.8 Visual Hierarchy (Eye Flow)
- **Description**: The visitor's eye naturally flows: headline -> subheadline -> CTA, in that order.
- **Why it matters**: If visual hierarchy is broken, visitors may miss the CTA or read elements in the wrong order, reducing comprehension.
- **How to fix**: Use size, color, and spacing to create a clear top-to-bottom or Z-pattern flow. The headline should be the largest text, CTA the most colorful element.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — visual hierarchy improvements lift conversions 5-10%.

### 1.9 No Distracting Navigation
- **Description**: The landing page has minimal or no top navigation. No links that lead away from the conversion goal.
- **Why it matters**: Every navigation link is an exit point. Landing pages with navigation have 15-25% lower conversion rates.
- **How to fix**: Remove the standard site navigation. Keep only the logo (non-clickable or linking to the same page) and the primary CTA.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — removing navigation can increase conversions 10-25%.

### 1.10 Page Loads in Under 3 Seconds
- **Description**: The above-the-fold content is fully rendered within 3 seconds on a standard connection.
- **Why it matters**: 53% of mobile visitors abandon pages that take longer than 3 seconds to load. Every 1-second delay costs ~7% in conversions.
- **How to fix**: Optimize images (WebP, compression), defer non-critical JS, inline critical CSS, use a CDN.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — speed improvements can lift conversions 7-20%.

### 1.11 No Auto-Playing Audio or Intrusive Media
- **Description**: No audio plays automatically. No full-screen modals on page load. No elements that block the content.
- **Why it matters**: Auto-playing audio and immediate popups are the fastest way to make a visitor leave. They signal disrespect for the visitor's time.
- **How to fix**: Remove auto-play audio. Delay any modals by at least 30 seconds or tie them to scroll depth or exit intent.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — removing intrusive elements can reduce bounce rate 20-40%.

### 1.12 Message Match with Traffic Source
- **Description**: The headline and offer match the ad, email, or link that brought the visitor to the page.
- **Why it matters**: Message mismatch is the #1 cause of high bounce rates. If the ad says "50% off" but the landing page says "Free trial", the visitor feels misled.
- **How to fix**: Create dedicated landing pages for each major traffic source. Mirror the ad headline, offer, and imagery.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — message match can increase conversions 20-50%.

### 1.13 Mobile Rendering Above the Fold
- **Description**: The hero section renders correctly on mobile devices (320px-428px width). CTA is thumb-reachable.
- **Why it matters**: 60%+ of traffic is mobile. If the hero is broken on mobile, you lose the majority of your visitors.
- **How to fix**: Test on multiple mobile viewports. Ensure headline is readable, CTA is tap-friendly (44x44px minimum), and no horizontal scrolling occurs.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — mobile optimization can increase mobile conversions 10-30%.

### 1.14 Clear Product Category
- **Description**: Within 5 seconds, a visitor can identify what category of product or service this is.
- **Why it matters**: If a visitor cannot categorize your product, they cannot evaluate it. "What is this?" must be answered instantly.
- **How to fix**: Include a descriptor tag or category label near the headline. "The analytics platform for marketing teams."
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — clarity of category reduces bounce rate 5-15%.

### 1.15 Emotional Resonance
- **Description**: The above-the-fold messaging creates an emotional response (excitement, relief, curiosity, aspiration).
- **Why it matters**: Decisions are made emotionally and justified rationally. If the hero does not trigger an emotional response, the visitor will not engage further.
- **How to fix**: Use power words, specific numbers, and language that reflects the visitor's internal dialogue.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — emotional resonance improvements can lift conversions 5-15%.

## Section 2: Headlines and Copy (10 Checkpoints)

### 2.1 Headline Specificity
- **Description**: The headline includes specific numbers, timeframes, or measurable outcomes.
- **Why it matters**: "Improve your marketing" is weak. "Increase leads by 47% in 30 days" is specific and credible.
- **How to fix**: Add a metric, percentage, timeframe, or dollar amount to the headline.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — specific headlines convert 20-30% better than vague ones.

### 2.2 Copy Readability (Grade Level)
- **Description**: Body copy is written at an 8th-grade reading level or lower (Flesch-Kincaid).
- **Why it matters**: Higher readability = higher comprehension = higher conversions. Most visitors skim, not read.
- **How to fix**: Use short sentences (under 20 words), simple words, and avoid jargon.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — readability improvements can lift conversions 5-10%.

### 2.3 Scannable Content Structure
- **Description**: Content uses headers, bullet points, bold text, and short paragraphs (1-3 sentences) for easy scanning.
- **Why it matters**: 79% of visitors scan rather than read. If key information is buried in paragraphs, it will be missed.
- **How to fix**: Break long paragraphs into bullets. Bold key phrases. Use descriptive subheadings.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — scannability improvements can lift time-on-page 20-40% and conversions 5-10%.

### 2.4 Feature-to-Benefit Translation
- **Description**: Every feature mentioned is paired with its benefit to the customer.
- **Why it matters**: Customers do not buy features; they buy outcomes. "Real-time sync" means nothing without "Your team always has the latest data."
- **How to fix**: For each feature, ask "So what?" until you reach the human benefit. Rewrite accordingly.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — benefit-focused copy typically lifts conversions 5-15%.

### 2.5 Consistent Voice and Tone
- **Description**: Copy maintains a consistent brand voice throughout the page (formal/casual, technical/accessible).
- **Why it matters**: Inconsistent tone feels unprofessional and reduces trust. It signals that different people wrote different sections without coordination.
- **How to fix**: Define a voice guideline (e.g., "professional but approachable, like a knowledgeable friend") and apply it uniformly.
- **Expected impact**: LOW — voice consistency primarily affects brand perception over time.

### 2.6 Power Words Usage
- **Description**: Copy uses proven conversion-driving words: free, new, proven, guaranteed, exclusive, instant, discover, easy, results.
- **Why it matters**: Certain words trigger emotional responses that drive action. They are shortcuts to the brain's decision-making center.
- **How to fix**: Audit copy for weak words and replace with power words where natural. Do not force them.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM — power words contribute incrementally to conversion lift.

### 2.7 Objection Handling in Copy
- **Description**: The page copy proactively addresses the top 3-5 objections visitors have before they reach the FAQ.
- **Why it matters**: Unanswered objections become reasons not to convert. Addressing them in the flow builds confidence.
- **How to fix**: Identify the top objections (from support tickets, sales calls, or competitor reviews) and weave answers into the relevant page sections.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — proactive objection handling can lift conversions 5-15%.

### 2.8 Action-Oriented Language
- **Description**: Copy uses active voice and action verbs throughout ("Get started", "Transform your workflow", "See the difference").
- **Why it matters**: Passive language ("Your workflow will be transformed") is weaker and less engaging than active language.
- **How to fix**: Replace passive constructions with active ones. Start sentences with verbs when possible.
- **Expected impact**: LOW — active language improves engagement incrementally.

### 2.9 No Jargon or Undefined Acronyms
- **Description**: The page avoids industry jargon and defines any acronyms used.
- **Why it matters**: If a visitor encounters a word they do not understand, it creates friction and makes them feel like the product is not for them.
- **How to fix**: Replace jargon with plain language. If a technical term is necessary, provide a brief explanation.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — removing jargon can reduce bounce rate 5-15%.

### 2.10 Urgency or Scarcity (When Appropriate)
- **Description**: If applicable, the copy creates a genuine reason to act now rather than later.
- **Why it matters**: Without urgency, visitors bookmark and forget. "I will come back later" is the death of conversions.
- **How to fix**: Add genuine deadlines, limited availability, or time-sensitive bonuses. Never use fake urgency.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — genuine urgency can increase conversions 10-30%.

## Section 3: Call to Action (10 Checkpoints)

### 3.1 Primary CTA is Unmistakably Clear
- **Description**: There is no ambiguity about what the primary action is that the visitor should take.
- **Why it matters**: Confusion about what to do next is the most common conversion killer.
- **How to fix**: Use one dominant CTA button. Make it larger and more colorful than any other clickable element.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH

### 3.2 CTA Repeated Throughout the Page
- **Description**: The primary CTA appears at least 3 times: above the fold, mid-page, and at the bottom.
- **Why it matters**: Different visitors are persuaded at different points. The CTA should be available when they are ready.
- **How to fix**: Add CTA buttons after the problem section, after social proof, and in the final section.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 3.3 CTA Micro-Copy Present
- **Description**: Small text near the CTA that reduces friction: "No credit card required", "Setup in 2 minutes", "Cancel anytime."
- **Why it matters**: Micro-copy addresses last-second objections at the moment of decision.
- **How to fix**: Add 1-2 lines of micro-copy directly below the CTA button.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — micro-copy typically lifts CTA clicks 5-15%.

### 3.4 CTA Loads Above the Fold
- **Description**: The first CTA is fully visible on the initial viewport without any scrolling.
- **Why it matters**: Some visitors are ready to convert immediately. If they have to scroll to find the CTA, you lose them.
- **How to fix**: Position the primary CTA within the hero section, not below it.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH

### 3.5 CTA Size is Appropriate
- **Description**: The CTA button is large enough to notice and click/tap easily. Minimum 44x44px for touch targets.
- **Why it matters**: Small buttons are hard to tap on mobile and easy to overlook on desktop.
- **How to fix**: Increase button padding (px-6 py-3 minimum), use text-base or larger font size.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 3.6 CTA Whitespace
- **Description**: The CTA has sufficient whitespace around it so it does not feel crowded.
- **Why it matters**: Whitespace draws the eye. A CTA surrounded by content is less noticeable than one with breathing room.
- **How to fix**: Add margin/padding around the CTA area. Separate it from surrounding content.
- **Expected impact**: LOW

### 3.7 CTA Direction (Where It Goes)
- **Description**: It is clear what happens when the visitor clicks the CTA. No surprise redirects.
- **Why it matters**: Uncertainty about what happens next creates hesitation.
- **How to fix**: Use descriptive button text and/or add a note: "Takes you to the signup page" or "Opens a 5-minute demo."
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 3.8 Secondary CTA Available (Non-Competing)
- **Description**: A lower-commitment alternative is available for visitors not ready for the primary action (e.g., "Watch a demo" alongside "Start free trial").
- **Why it matters**: Not every visitor is ready to convert immediately. A secondary option captures those who need more information.
- **How to fix**: Add a text link or ghost button near the primary CTA for a lower-commitment action.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 3.9 CTA Accessibility
- **Description**: The CTA is accessible: sufficient color contrast (WCAG AA), keyboard navigable, proper ARIA labels.
- **Why it matters**: Inaccessible CTAs exclude visitors with disabilities and may violate legal requirements.
- **How to fix**: Ensure 4.5:1 contrast ratio for text, add focus styles, use semantic button elements.
- **Expected impact**: LOW for conversion rate, HIGH for compliance and inclusivity.

### 3.10 CTA A/B Test History
- **Description**: The CTA copy, color, and placement have been tested within the past 6 months.
- **Why it matters**: CTAs are the highest-leverage element to test. Untested CTAs are leaving conversions on the table.
- **How to fix**: Set up an A/B test on the CTA copy, color, or placement as the first optimization action.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — CTA tests frequently produce 10-30% lifts.

## Section 4: Social Proof (8 Checkpoints)

### 4.1 Multiple Types of Social Proof
- **Description**: The page includes at least 3 different types: testimonials, logos, metrics, case studies, ratings, or media mentions.
- **Why it matters**: Different visitors are persuaded by different types of proof. Layering creates compound credibility.
- **How to fix**: Add at least 3 of: customer testimonials, logo bar, metric counters, case study snippets, third-party ratings, media mentions.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH

### 4.2 Testimonials Include Results
- **Description**: Testimonials reference specific, measurable outcomes ("increased revenue by 47%", "saved 10 hours per week").
- **Why it matters**: Vague testimonials ("Great product!") carry little weight. Specific results are credible and persuasive.
- **How to fix**: Reach out to customers for metrics-based testimonials. Guide them with "What specific result did you achieve?"
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 4.3 Testimonials Include Attribution
- **Description**: Each testimonial includes the person's full name, job title, company name, and photo.
- **Why it matters**: Anonymous testimonials feel fake. Full attribution signals authenticity.
- **How to fix**: Add real photos, full names, and company details. Ask customers for permission.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 4.4 Logo Bar is Recognizable
- **Description**: The logo bar includes companies the target audience would recognize and respect.
- **Why it matters**: Unrecognizable logos do not create social proof. They may even reduce credibility ("Are these made up?").
- **How to fix**: Feature your best-known customers. If you lack big names, use industry-specific recognizable companies or remove the logo bar.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 4.5 Social Proof is Relevant to the Audience
- **Description**: The testimonials and logos are from companies or individuals similar to the target visitor.
- **Why it matters**: A Fortune 500 testimonial does not persuade a startup founder. Relevance matters more than prestige.
- **How to fix**: Segment testimonials by audience. Show the most relevant proof to each visitor segment.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH

### 4.6 Social Proof Placement is Strategic
- **Description**: Social proof appears at decision points: near CTAs, after the value proposition, and before the pricing section.
- **Why it matters**: Social proof is most effective when it appears at the moment of highest doubt.
- **How to fix**: Place testimonials or metric counters directly above or below CTA sections.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 4.7 Third-Party Validation Present
- **Description**: Reviews from independent platforms (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot) or media coverage are displayed.
- **Why it matters**: Third-party validation is more credible than self-reported testimonials because the company cannot control the platform.
- **How to fix**: If you have good ratings on review platforms, embed badges or review widgets.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 4.8 Social Proof is Current
- **Description**: Testimonials and case studies reference recent results (within the past 12 months).
- **Why it matters**: Outdated social proof suggests the product may no longer deliver the same results.
- **How to fix**: Update testimonials annually. Add dates or "as of [year]" labels.
- **Expected impact**: LOW

## Section 5: Trust and Security (8 Checkpoints)

### 5.1 Trust Badges Near Forms and CTAs
- **Description**: Security badges, guarantees, or trust seals are displayed near form fields and CTA buttons.
- **Why it matters**: Trust signals at the point of action reduce last-second anxiety.
- **How to fix**: Add recognizable trust badges (SSL, SOC 2, money-back guarantee) within 50px of the CTA.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — trust badges near CTAs can lift conversions 5-15%.

### 5.2 Privacy Policy Linked
- **Description**: A link to the privacy policy is present near any form that collects personal data.
- **Why it matters**: Required by GDPR and good practice. Its absence creates anxiety about data usage.
- **How to fix**: Add a small text link below forms: "We respect your privacy. Read our privacy policy."
- **Expected impact**: LOW for conversions, HIGH for compliance.

### 5.3 Guarantee or Refund Policy Visible
- **Description**: If selling a product, the guarantee or refund policy is clearly stated on the page.
- **Why it matters**: A clear guarantee reduces purchase anxiety. Companies that display guarantees prominently see higher conversion rates.
- **How to fix**: Add a guarantee section with a shield icon and clear terms: "30-day money-back guarantee. No questions asked."
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — guarantees can lift paid conversions 5-20%.

### 5.4 Contact Information Accessible
- **Description**: Real contact information (email, phone, or chat) is available on the page.
- **Why it matters**: The absence of contact info signals a lack of accountability. Visitors think "What if I need help?"
- **How to fix**: Add a footer or sidebar with contact details. Live chat widgets are particularly effective.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 5.5 Professional Design Quality
- **Description**: The page looks professional, modern, and well-maintained. No broken images, misaligned elements, or outdated design patterns.
- **Why it matters**: Design quality is a trust signal. Visitors judge credibility by visual appearance within milliseconds.
- **How to fix**: Audit for broken elements, inconsistent spacing, and outdated components. Refresh to current design standards.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM — design quality affects trust and perceived value.

### 5.6 HTTPS Enabled
- **Description**: The page is served over HTTPS with a valid SSL certificate.
- **Why it matters**: Non-HTTPS pages show "Not Secure" in the browser, immediately destroying trust.
- **How to fix**: Install an SSL certificate and redirect all HTTP traffic to HTTPS.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — "Not Secure" warnings can reduce conversions 20-40%.

### 5.7 No Broken Links or 404 Errors
- **Description**: All links on the page work correctly and lead to valid destinations.
- **Why it matters**: Broken links signal neglect and reduce trust. They also frustrate visitors trying to learn more.
- **How to fix**: Test every link on the page. Set up automated link checking.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 5.8 Clear Company Identity
- **Description**: The visitor can easily identify who is behind the product (company name, logo, "About" information).
- **Why it matters**: Anonymous products feel risky. Knowing who is behind the product creates accountability.
- **How to fix**: Include company logo, brief description, and optionally a team photo or founding story.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

## Section 6: Forms (10 Checkpoints)

### 6.1 Minimum Required Fields
- **Description**: The form asks for only the information absolutely necessary for the conversion goal.
- **Why it matters**: Every additional field reduces form completion rate by ~5-10%. Ask for what you need, nothing more.
- **How to fix**: Remove optional fields. If you need them later, ask in a follow-up form after conversion.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — reducing from 10 fields to 5 can double form completions.

### 6.2 Appropriate Input Types
- **Description**: Form inputs use the correct HTML input types (email, tel, url, number) for mobile keyboard optimization.
- **Why it matters**: Correct input types trigger appropriate mobile keyboards, reducing friction and errors.
- **How to fix**: Audit every input field and set the correct type attribute.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 6.3 Clear Labels and Placeholders
- **Description**: Every field has a visible label (not just a placeholder) and helpful placeholder text.
- **Why it matters**: Placeholder-only labels disappear on focus, leaving the user unsure what to enter.
- **How to fix**: Use persistent labels above each field. Use placeholders for format examples ("e.g., john@company.com").
- **Expected impact**: LOW

### 6.4 Inline Validation
- **Description**: Form validation happens in real-time as the user fills out each field, not only on submission.
- **Why it matters**: Inline validation reduces form errors by 22% and increases completion rates.
- **How to fix**: Add onBlur or onChange validation for each field with clear error messages.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 6.5 Error Messages are Helpful
- **Description**: Error messages explain what is wrong and how to fix it ("Please enter a valid email address" not "Invalid input").
- **Why it matters**: Unclear error messages frustrate users and increase form abandonment.
- **How to fix**: Rewrite all error messages to be specific, friendly, and actionable.
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 6.6 Multi-Step for Long Forms
- **Description**: Forms with 5+ fields are broken into 2-4 steps with a progress indicator.
- **Why it matters**: Multi-step forms convert 86% better than single-step forms with the same fields. They feel less overwhelming.
- **How to fix**: Split the form into logical steps. Show progress. Allow going back.
- **Expected impact**: HIGH — for forms with 5+ fields, multi-step is almost always better.

### 6.7 Submit Button Text
- **Description**: The form submit button uses benefit-driven or action-specific text, not "Submit".
- **Why it matters**: "Submit" is the default and feels corporate. "Get my free report" or "Create my account" is motivating.
- **How to fix**: Replace "Submit" with the outcome of submitting.
- **Expected impact**: MEDIUM

### 6.8 Form is Visually Distinct
- **Description**: The form area is visually separated from the rest of the page (card, border, different background).
- **Why it matters**: A distinct form area signals "action zone" and draws the eye to the conversion point.
- **How to fix**: Add a card container with a border, shadow, or contrasting background color.
- **Expected impact**: LOW

### 6.9 Autofill Support
- **Description**: The form supports browser autofill with correct autocomplete attributes.
- **Why it matters**: Autofill reduces effort to nearly zero, significantly increasing completion rates.
- **How to fix**: Add autocomplete attributes to all fields (autocomplete="email", "name", "tel", etc.).
- **Expected impact**: LOW-MEDIUM

### 6.10 Thank You / Confirmation
- **Description**: After form submission, the user sees a clear confirmation message or is redirected to a thank you page.
- **Why it matters**: Without confirmation, users are unsure if their submission went through and may submit again or leave frustrated.
- **How to fix**: Show an inline success message or redirect to a dedicated thank you page with next steps.
- **Expected impact**: LOW for conversion rate, HIGH for user experience and lead quality.
